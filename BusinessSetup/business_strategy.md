# Recommended structure

For your case, I would start with a **UK limited company**, not as a long-term sole trader.

Why:

* You want to keep a full-time job while building this up, then transition later.
* A limited company is usually a better fit for B2B consulting credibility, liability separation, future subcontracting, and later saleability or scaling. GOV.UK says directors of limited companies must keep records, file accounts, submit the company tax return, and meet Companies House obligations. ([GOV.UK][1])
* Sole trader is simpler, but from **6 April 2026**, sole traders and landlords with qualifying income over £50,000 must use Making Tax Digital for Income Tax, and the regime extends further later. ([GOV.UK][2])

My recommendation:

* **Phase 1:** form a limited company now.
* **Phase 2:** do side work through the company.
* **Phase 3:** move from employee income dominance to company income dominance.
* **Phase 4:** leave the job only when revenue is stable enough.

# Legal and compliance setup in the UK

For a UK limited company, the main practical setup is:

* register the company with Companies House
* directors must comply with filing and record-keeping responsibilities
* Companies House online incorporation costs **£100**, and Companies House fees changed from **1 February 2026**; identity verification is also being introduced/required in the new regime. ([GOV.UK][3])
* register for Corporation Tax and consider VAT; VAT registration is mandatory above **£90,000** taxable turnover and voluntary registration is possible below that. ([GOV.UK][4])
* if you process personal data, you may need to pay the ICO data protection fee; many small organisations pay **£52** or **£78**, unless exempt. ([ICO][5])
* consider trade mark registration if brand value starts to matter. ([GOV.UK][6])

You should also have:

* accountant
* business bank account
* bookkeeping software
* professional indemnity insurance
* terms of business / MSA / SOW templates
* privacy policy
* NDA template
* contractor/subcontractor agreement template

HMRC guidance states premiums on professional negligence indemnity policies are allowable business deductions. ([GOV.UK][7])

# Employment-risk strategy while you still have a job

This part matters a lot.

Before doing any side work:

* check your employment contract carefully
* look for exclusivity, conflict of interest, IP ownership, confidentiality, non-compete, moonlighting restrictions, and approval requirements

ACAS says contracts can include terms restricting a worker’s actions, including working elsewhere or creating conflicts. ([Acas][8])

Your safety rules:

* no client overlap with employer’s customers/competitors
* no use of employer equipment, code, contacts database, or working hours
* separate laptop, email, phone number, storage, and accounts
* separate IP
* written permission if your contract requires it
* keep records proving side work is separate

# IR35 / tax strategy

If your company takes contract work, IR35/off-payroll can apply on a contract-by-contract basis. GOV.UK states the rules apply contract by contract, and where relevant the client determines status and issues a status determination statement. ([GOV.UK][9])

Practical strategy:

* prefer advisory, diagnostics, workshops, architecture reviews, and statement-of-work outcomes early
* avoid looking like disguised employment
* avoid one-client-full-time arrangements unless status is clear
* get accountant input on every substantial contract
